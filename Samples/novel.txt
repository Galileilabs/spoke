The house at the end of Calloway Street had been dark for eleven years. Elena knew this because she had counted every one of them, the way a prisoner counts days scratched into a wall. She stood at the gate now, her hand resting on the rusted latch, the metal so cold it burned through her gloves.

The garden had surrendered long ago. What had once been her mother's roses were now brittle stalks clawing upward like the fingers of something buried. The walkway was cracked, split by roots that had been patient enough to win their war against concrete.

She pushed the gate open. It didn't creak. It groaned, low and reluctant, as if it remembered her and wished she hadn't come back.

The front door was unlocked. She had expected that. Her brother Martin had never believed in locking things. Locks, he used to say, only keep out the people who weren't going to come in anyway.

Inside, the air was thick and sweet, a smell like old paper and something faintly chemical. Dust covered everything in a fine even layer, as democratic as snowfall. She ran a finger along the hallway table and left a dark trail on the wood beneath.

"Hello?" she called, though she knew no one would answer.

Her voice came back to her from the stairs, from the kitchen, from rooms she couldn't yet see. The house absorbed it and returned it slightly changed, the way the ocean changes a stone.

She found Martin's study at the back of the house. His desk was buried under stacks of notebooks, their spines cracked, their pages swollen with humidity. A coffee cup sat near the edge, the liquid inside long evaporated, leaving a dark ring like a small eclipse.

Elena sat in his chair. The leather exhaled beneath her weight. She opened the nearest notebook and recognized his handwriting immediately. Tight, slanted, impatient. He wrote the way he spoke, in bursts, as if the words might escape before he could pin them down.

"I've been thinking about what you said at the funeral," she whispered to the empty room. "That we were never really a family. Just people who happened to share a kitchen."

She turned another page. A photograph slipped out and landed face-down on the desk. She picked it up. The two of them, eight and twelve, standing in this very garden when the roses were alive and the gate didn't groan. Her mother had taken the picture. You could tell because the framing was slightly off, the horizon tilted, the way it always was when she held the camera.

Elena placed the photograph back inside the notebook and closed it. She sat for a long while, listening to the house settle around her, its small sounds of wood and wind, the ticking of a clock she couldn't locate.

When she finally stood, she took nothing with her. She walked back down the hallway, past the table with her fingerprint in the dust, through the groaning gate, and into the street where the evening light was turning everything the color of bruised fruit.

She did not look back. But she left the gate open.
