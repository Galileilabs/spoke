The relationship between entropy and information theory represents one of the most profound intellectual bridges of the twentieth century. When Claude Shannon published his landmark 1948 paper, he borrowed the term entropy from thermodynamics, but he redefined it in purely mathematical terms. In Shannon's framework, entropy measures the average uncertainty associated with a random variable. Given a discrete random variable X with possible outcomes x-one through x-n, each occurring with probability p of x-i, the entropy H of X equals the negative sum of p of x-i times the logarithm base two of p of x-i. This quantity reaches its maximum when all outcomes are equally likely, and it collapses to zero when the outcome is certain.

Now, why does this matter? Consider a communication channel. The fundamental problem of communication, as Shannon framed it, is reproducing at one point a message selected at another point. The entropy of the source tells us the minimum average number of bits required per symbol to encode messages without loss. This is Shannon's source coding theorem, sometimes called the noiseless coding theorem. It establishes a hard lower bound. No lossless compression scheme can beat it.

The connection to thermodynamics is more than superficial. Boltzmann's entropy, S equals k-B times the natural logarithm of omega, counts the number of microstates consistent with a macrostate. Shannon's entropy counts the effective number of distinguishable messages. Both measure a kind of multiplicity. Jaynes formalized this connection in 1957, arguing that statistical mechanics itself is a form of statistical inference. The equilibrium distribution maximizes Shannon entropy subject to known constraints, such as average energy. This maximum entropy principle provides a principled way to assign probabilities when information is incomplete.

Let us examine the implications for data compression more carefully. Huffman coding, developed in 1952, constructs prefix-free codes that approach the entropy bound symbol by symbol. Arithmetic coding goes further, encoding entire sequences and achieving rates arbitrarily close to entropy. Modern compression algorithms like the Lempel-Ziv family take a different approach, exploiting statistical regularities without explicit probability models, yet their asymptotic performance still converges to the entropy rate of the source.

The extension to continuous random variables introduces differential entropy, defined as the negative integral of f of x times the logarithm of f of x. Unlike discrete entropy, differential entropy can be negative and depends on the coordinate system. This creates subtle difficulties when applying information-theoretic arguments to analog signals, which is why the mutual information, the reduction in uncertainty about one variable given knowledge of another, becomes the more robust quantity in continuous settings.

Finally, consider channel capacity. Shannon's channel coding theorem states that reliable communication is possible at any rate below capacity, defined as the maximum mutual information between input and output, and impossible above it. This existence proof stunned engineers, because it guaranteed error-free transmission without specifying how to achieve it. Decades of coding theory, from convolutional codes to turbo codes to low-density parity-check codes, have been the slow practical pursuit of Shannon's theoretical promise. The gap between theory and practice has narrowed to fractions of a decibel.
